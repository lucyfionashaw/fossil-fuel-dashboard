Coal's paradox: Coal provides ~26% of primary energy but produces ~41% of energy-related CO2 emissions. This is because coal emits roughly twice as much CO2 per unit of energy as natural gas, and significantly more than oil. Understanding coal's outsized emissions footprint is essential context for any analysis of fossil fuel economics.

Methodology & Sources

Primary energy: Based on the substitution method used by the Energy Institute Statistical Review (formerly BP), where nuclear and renewables are converted to primary energy equivalents at ~38% thermal efficiency. Total global primary energy consumption in 2024 was approximately 620 EJ. Shares: oil ~31%, coal ~26%, gas ~24%, hydro ~7%, renewables (wind/solar/bio) ~8%, nuclear ~4%.

Electricity: Global electricity generation in 2024 was approximately 30,000 TWh (Ember Global Electricity Review 2025). Coal: ~35%, gas: ~22%, hydro: ~15%, nuclear: ~9%, wind: ~8%, solar: ~6%, other renewables: ~3%, oil: ~2%.

CO2 emissions: Energy-related CO2 emissions from fossil fuel combustion and industrial processes. 2024 estimate: 37.6 Gt CO2 (IEA). Coal: ~15.5 Gt (41%), oil: ~11.1 Gt (30%), gas: ~7.9 Gt (21%), industrial processes: ~3.1 Gt (8%).

CO2 intensity factors: IPCC AR6 default emission factors. Coal (anthracite/bituminous): ~95 kg CO2/GJ; oil: ~73 kg CO2/GJ; natural gas: ~56 kg CO2/GJ.

Country coal shares: Ember country-level electricity data (2024). Change vs 2019 calculated from same source. Some small countries with very high coal shares (e.g., Kosovo, North Macedonia) omitted from top-20 chart due to small absolute generation.

CO2 intensity per kWh: Accounts for thermal efficiency of power plants, not just fuel carbon content. Coal plants operate at ~33% efficiency (lignite) to ~40% (supercritical), meaning only a third to two-fifths of coal's energy becomes electricity. Gas CCGT plants reach ~55% efficiency. Lifecycle emissions for renewables and nuclear include manufacturing, construction, and decommissioning. Sources: IPCC AR6 WG3, IEA.

Coal tonnes per MWh: Varies by coal quality (calorific value in kcal/kg) and plant age/technology. India's predominantly sub-bituminous domestic coal (~3,500–4,500 kcal/kg) and older subcritical plants require ~0.72 tonnes/MWh, while Japan's ultra-supercritical plants burning high-grade imported coal (~6,000+ kcal/kg) need only ~0.40 tonnes/MWh. Sources: IEA, national statistics.
