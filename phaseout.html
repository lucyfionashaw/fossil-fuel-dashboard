The coal phaseout paradox: While 56 countries have joined the Powering Past Coal Alliance and over 270 GW has been retired since 2010, these commitments cover a small fraction of global coal capacity. China and India, which together operate ~1,400 GW, have no phaseout dates. New construction, overwhelmingly in Asia, continues to outpace retirements globally, resulting in a net increase of ~233 GW since 2015.

Methodology & Sources

Coal capacity data: Operating, under-construction, planned/announced, and retired coal power plant capacity from the Global Energy Monitor (GEM) Global Coal Plant Tracker, January 2025 update. Capacity figures are in gross nameplate capacity (GW). Retired capacity covers plants closed from 2010 onwards.

Phaseout commitments: A country is classified as "committed" if its national government has made a public statement, enacted legislation, or signed an international agreement (e.g., PPCA) specifying a date to end unabated coal power generation. Conditional commitments (e.g., subject to international finance) are noted. "No date" means the country has not set a coal phaseout target, even if it has climate pledges that imply future coal reduction.

PPCA membership: The Powering Past Coal Alliance is a coalition of national and sub-national governments, businesses, and organisations committed to phasing out unabated coal power. As of 2024, the PPCA has 56 national government members plus many sub-national and institutional members. However, no major coal power country (China, India, USA, Indonesia) is a member.

Coal generation trend: Regional coal electricity generation data from Ember Global Electricity Review (2025) and the Energy Institute Statistical Review (2025, formerly BP). Regions: China, India, Rest of Asia (Japan, South Korea, Indonesia, Vietnam, etc.), Europe (EU + UK + Turkey), Americas (USA, Canada, Latin America), Other (Africa, Middle East, Oceania).

Retirements vs new builds: Annual GW of coal capacity commissioned (reaching commercial operation) vs retired (permanently closed) globally, from Global Energy Monitor. Note that "commissioned" includes plants that were under construction for years; the commissioning year is when they began generating power.
