What counts as "foreign investment": This page tracks cross-border finance for coal projects — including direct foreign investment (equity stakes), export credit agency (ECA) loans, development finance institution (DFI) lending, and commercial bank syndicated loans where the capital originates from a different country than the project. Domestic investment (e.g., India's Coal India spending) is excluded.

Key Policy Changes & Divestment Decisions

Major institutional and government commitments to end overseas coal finance.

2013
World Bank ends coal financing
World Bank Group announces it will no longer finance greenfield coal power projects except in rare circumstances of extreme energy poverty.
2013
US & Nordic countries restrict export credit for coal
Obama administration limits US Ex-Im Bank coal financing. Nordic countries follow with ECA restrictions on coal power abroad.
2015
OECD restricts export credits for coal plants
OECD Arrangement restricts export credit agency financing for the least efficient coal-fired power plants (sub-critical technology). Tightened further in 2017 and 2021.
2015
Paris Agreement adopted
196 parties commit to limiting warming to well below 2°C. Accelerates ESG-driven divestment from coal by development banks, pension funds, and commercial lenders.
2017
France bans public coal finance abroad
France becomes the first major economy to legislate a ban on all public export finance for coal projects, taking effect across all French institutions.
2019
European Investment Bank ends fossil fuel finance
EIB announces it will stop financing all fossil fuel projects (coal, oil, gas) from end of 2021. Largest multilateral development bank climate policy to date.
2020
Major commercial banks exit coal lending
Over 100 major global financial institutions adopt coal exclusion policies. HSBC, Deutsche Bank, UniCredit, Standard Chartered restrict new coal project finance.
2021 (Apr)
South Korea pledges to end overseas coal finance
President Moon announces Korea will stop all public financing for overseas coal power. KEXIM and Korea Development Bank had been major coal financiers in SE Asia.
2021 (Jun)
Japan pledges to stop overseas coal finance
At G7 Summit, Japan commits to ending overseas unabated coal finance by end of 2021. JBIC had been the world's largest public coal financier alongside China.
2021 (Sep)
China pledges to stop overseas coal power
President Xi Jinping announces at UNGA that China will stop building new coal-fired power projects abroad. Affects ~$50B pipeline of BRI coal plants across Asia and Africa.
2021 (Nov)
COP26 Glasgow: 40+ countries pledge coal phaseout
Global Coal to Clean Power Transition Statement commits signatories to phase out coal power domestically and end international coal finance.
2022
G7 ends public finance for unabated fossil fuels abroad
G7 Elmau Summit commits to ending public finance for unabated overseas fossil fuel projects by end of 2022, broadening beyond coal to oil and gas.
2023–2024
Implementation and loopholes
Pledges translate into measurable decline. However, some Chinese-financed projects continue under pre-pledge commitments. Japan allows "ammonia co-firing" exceptions. Enforcement varies.

Methodology & Sources

Data Sources

  • Global Energy Monitor (GEM): Global Coal Finance Tracker (primary dataset). Tracks individual coal project-level finance commitments including investor identity, amount, date, and project status. Updated quarterly.
  • Boston University Global Development Policy Center: China's Global Energy Finance database. Tracks Chinese overseas energy investment via China Development Bank (CDB) and Export-Import Bank of China (CHEXIM).
  • OECD: Export credit data on coal-related ECA financing commitments and volumes by member country.
  • Oil Change International: Public finance for energy database (Shift the Subsidies). Tracks bilateral and multilateral development finance for fossil fuels.
  • Urgewald Global Coal Exit List: Identifies financial institutions and their coal-related exposures across the value chain.
  • IEA World Energy Investment: Annual investment data by fuel type and region, including estimated cross-border flows.
  • IEEFA (Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis): Reports on individual country and project-level coal finance trends.

Methodology Notes

  • Scope: Cross-border finance only. Domestic investment (e.g., Indian banks financing Indian coal plants) is excluded. Includes equity investment, debt (loans and bonds), export credit guarantees, and development finance grants.
  • Valuation: Project values are based on total committed finance at the time of financial close or announcement, not disbursed amounts. Values are nominal USD. Some projects have multiple foreign investors; total is allocated proportionally where breakdown is available, otherwise attributed to the lead financier.
  • Time period: Trend data covers 2015–2024. Cumulative figures cover 2013–2023 to capture the full post-World Bank restriction period.
  • Headline annual figure: The ~$17B figure for 2023 reflects new commitments entering the GEM tracker, down from ~$35B in 2015. Some variation across sources due to different scope definitions (e.g., BU GDP Center includes Chinese policy bank loans only).
  • Limitations: Private-sector commercial bank lending is undercounted as syndicated loan data is often confidential. Chinese investment data relies on public announcements and may miss smaller transactions. Project cancellations/suspensions are tracked but may lag actual status.
