How concentrated are coal jobs?

Coal employment splits into two very different geographies. Mining is tied to geology — packed into a few Appalachian and Powder River Basin counties. Power generation follows the grid and population, so it is far more dispersed. Switch the map to “% of all jobs” to see local dependence directly.

⛏️ Coal mining Fuels · 64,412 jobs
  • Half of all US coal-mining jobs sit in just 24 counties; 80% in 65.
  • Campbell County, WY (Powder River Basin) alone holds 1 in 12 (8.1%); the top 10 counties hold 32%.
  • West Virginia, Kentucky & Pennsylvania together = half the national total; WV alone is 29%.
  • In Martin County, KY, coal mining is 82% of all private-sector jobs; 10 counties are above 25%.
⚡ Coal power Generation · 60,439 jobs
  • It takes 51 counties to reach half of all coal-power jobs; 148 for 80%.
  • No single county tops 2.9% (Hamilton County, OH); the top 10 hold just 20%.
  • Most dispersed at state level too — top state Ohio is only 13%; the top 3 states reach just 25%.
  • Locally lighter: Apache County, AZ is the most exposed at 26% of all jobs; only 1 county exceeds 25%.

Clustering stats use counties with a disclosed (≥10) coal workforce, which capture 98% (mining) / 95% (power) of national jobs. “% of all jobs” uses total private-sector employment from the Census County Business Patterns 2022 (excludes government, self-employed & farm labour), matched for 3,181 of 3,193 counties.

About this map

Source: U.S. Energy and Employment Report (USEER) 2025, county-level employment estimates. Per your request, sectors are bucketed as follows:

  • Electric Power Generation — split into components (Solar, Wind, Hydroelectric, Low-Impact Hydro, Natural Gas, Coal, Oil & Other FF, Other).
  • Fuels — split into components (Coal, Petroleum & Other FF, Natural Gas, Woody Biomass, Corn Ethanol, Other).
  • Transmission, Distribution & Storage — combined into a single bucket.
  • Energy Efficiency — combined into a single bucket.
  • Motor Vehicles — single category.

State view uses authoritative state totals from the USEER 2025 State Reports (Validation sheet for sector totals; sub-sector cut for component categories). This is the cleanest view for spotting geographic patterns.

How suppressed county values are imputed

USEER suppresses small county counts, reporting them only as “<10”. To reconcile the county build-up to the known state totals (~8.5M jobs nationally, vs ~8.28M if suppressed cells are treated as zero/placeholder), we impute each suppressed cell as follows, per state × category:

  1. Take the state total T for that category and subtract the sum R of all reported (non-suppressed) counties.
  2. Distribute the shortfall (T − R) evenly across the suppressed counties in that state — i.e. each gets the average of the missing jobs.

Imputed county values are flagged as “est.” in tooltips. Caveat: where a state has only one or two suppressed counties but a sizeable gap, the per-county estimate can exceed 10 — this absorbs not just hidden employment but also rounding/methodology differences between the county and state datasets, so treat large imputed county values as approximate. Component categories are anchored to the matching state sub-sector (e.g. county Hydroelectric → state “Traditional Hydro”; county “Other” generation absorbs the state’s Nuclear, which has no county split). The state file reports a single “Traditional Hydro” figure, which we split into Hydroelectric and Low-Impact Hydro using each state’s county-level proportions, so the two sum to the authoritative EPG total rather than adding on top of it. The national total reconciles exactly to the USEER authoritative figure of 8,466,698 jobs.

“Jobs” vs “Share of energy jobs”

The Jobs metric maps the raw job count. The Share of energy jobs metric divides the selected category by the geography’s total energy employment (e.g. “what % of this county’s energy jobs are coal”). Note the denominator is total energy employment, not all-industry employment — USEER is an energy dataset and does not include county-wide payrolls, so this is not the energy share of the whole local economy. For the “All sectors” category, share instead shows each area’s contribution to the national energy-jobs total. The share view is the best way to compare states/counties on a like-for-like basis without large places dominating.

Are counties comparable in size?

No — and this matters for reading the map. US counties vary enormously in both land area and population. Western counties (e.g. in California, Nevada, Arizona) are geographically huge, while eastern states like West Virginia, Kentucky and Virginia are carved into many small counties. So a coal county in WV is a tiny shape and a coal county in the West is a large one, even for the same number of jobs — this is the classic choropleth “area bias”, where big sparsely-populated counties draw the eye regardless of headcount. Two defences are built in: the State view removes within-state granularity entirely, and the Share of energy jobs metric normalises away from absolute size. When hunting for coal hotspots, compare these views rather than trusting the size of a colored blob alone.
