Direct vs Implicit Subsidies: What Counts?

The IMF's $7 trillion figure includes both fiscal support and the cost of unpriced environmental and health damage. These are fundamentally different policy mechanisms.

Direct (Explicit) Subsidies — $1.3T

Government budgetary transfers and tax breaks that directly reduce fossil fuel costs below market price.

  • Consumer price subsidies: regulated fuel prices below international benchmarks (dominant in Middle East, North Africa, South/SE Asia)
  • Producer subsidies: capital grants, R&D support, exploration incentives, royalty relief
  • Tax preferences: accelerated depreciation, fuel tax exemptions, VAT relief for energy inputs
  • SOE cross-subsidies: state-owned companies selling below cost (e.g., Pertamina, NNPC)

Implicit Subsidies (Externalities) — $5.7T

Costs imposed on society that are not reflected in fossil fuel prices — the gap between what consumers pay and the true social cost.

  • Local air pollution: PM2.5, SO2, NOx causing premature deaths, respiratory illness (~$2.8T from coal alone)
  • Climate damage: CO2 emissions not priced at social cost of carbon (~$1.8T at $60/tCO2)
  • Vehicle externalities: congestion, road damage, accidents (~$0.6T)
  • Foregone consumption tax revenue: fuel taxed below efficient levels (~$0.5T)
Why the numbers differ: The IEA tracks direct fiscal subsidies ($1.3T). The IMF adds the cost of unpriced externalities ($5.7T) to arrive at $7T. Both are valid frameworks — the IEA figure measures government fiscal cost, the IMF figure measures total societal cost. This dashboard presents both, clearly labelled.

Interpretation note: High subsidy-per-worker ratios do not mean workers receive these sums. The metric shows how much public fiscal + societal cost is associated with each fossil fuel job. Countries with high consumption, few workers, and large externalities show the highest ratios.

Subsidy Reform Tracker

G20 pledged to phase out "inefficient" fossil fuel subsidies in 2009. Status as of 2024.

G20 scorecard: 15 years after the Pittsburgh pledge, G20 fossil fuel subsidies have increased from ~$500B (2009) to ~$1.3T (2022). Only a handful of countries have implemented significant reforms, and several reversed course during the 2021–2022 energy crisis.

Notable Reforms

Reformed
India (2014–2023): Eliminated diesel subsidy (2014), deregulated petrol, LPG subsidy targeted via DBT (Ujjwala). Fuel taxes raised significantly. Savings: ~$15B/yr.
Reformed
Indonesia (2014–2015): President Jokowi eliminated petrol subsidies, capped diesel subsidy. Freed $15.6B for infrastructure and social spending.
Reformed
UAE (2015): Moved to market-linked fuel pricing. Eliminated petrol and diesel subsidies. First Gulf state to fully deregulate.
Reformed
Egypt (2014–2024): Phased reduction over 10 years. Fuel subsidies fell from $18B (2013) to ~$3B (2024). IMF-supported programme.
Partial
Saudi Arabia (2016–2018): Raised domestic fuel and electricity prices as part of Vision 2030. But prices still well below international levels.
Partial
China (2013–): Coal price deregulation, electricity market reforms. But coal power still receives capacity payments and preferential dispatch.

Stalled or Reversed

Stalled
United States: $20B+/yr in producer tax preferences (IDCs, percentage depletion). IRA added clean energy credits but did not remove fossil fuel provisions.
Stalled
Russia: Domestic price controls keep gas at ~$1.70/MMBtu vs $10+ export price. ~$60B/yr in implicit consumer subsidies. No reform planned.
Reversed
EU (2021–2023): Energy crisis led to massive new consumer support: fuel tax cuts, price caps, windfall levies. ~$200B in emergency fossil fuel subsidies.
Reversed
Nigeria (2023): President Tinubu ended petrol subsidy (May 2023), then partially reinstated price controls after public protests. Reform incomplete.
Stalled
Iran: Fuel prices set at ~$0.03/litre (among world's lowest). ~$80B/yr in consumer subsidies. Subsidy reform attempts (2019) met with protests.
Stalled
Argentina: Energy subsidies ~3% of GDP. Multiple reform attempts reversed by successive governments.

Methodology & Sources

Total subsidies (IMF): IMF Working Paper "IMF Fossil Fuel Subsidies Data: 2023 Update" (Parry, Black, Zhunussova, 2023). Total = explicit subsidies + implicit subsidies. Implicit = undercharging for supply costs + environmental/health externalities + foregone consumption tax revenue. Base year: 2022 with projections to 2023. Covers 170 countries, all fossil fuels.

Direct subsidies (IEA/OECD): IEA World Energy Outlook 2023 and OECD Inventory of Support Measures for Fossil Fuels (2023). Consumer price subsidies measured as price gap (domestic price vs. international reference). Producer subsidies include budgetary transfers, tax expenditures, and below-market provision of goods/services.

Health costs: Lancet Commission on Pollution and Health (2022 update). Coal air pollution mortality estimate based on Global Burden of Disease study, IHME. $2.8T figure uses WHO Value of Statistical Life methodology applied to estimated 1.2M premature deaths/yr from coal PM2.5.

Employment data: ILO (formal employment), national statistics, IEA World Energy Employment report (2023), Global Energy Monitor coal mine databases. Fossil fuel employment includes extraction, processing, transport, and power generation. Country-specific notes in table.

Per worker calculation: Total IMF subsidy for country divided by estimated total fossil fuel workforce. This is a ratio metric, not a per-worker benefit. Workers do not receive these amounts; the figure reflects the societal cost associated with maintaining each fossil fuel job.

Clean energy investment: IEA World Energy Investment 2024, BNEF Global Energy Transition Investment Trends 2024. Includes renewables, EVs, batteries, nuclear, hydrogen, CCS, grids, and efficiency.

Reform tracker: IMF Subsidy Reform Database, IISD Global Subsidies Initiative, World Bank ESMAP. Status as of late 2024.
