Types of Coal Taxation

Countries tax coal through multiple overlapping instruments. This page aims to capture the total fiscal burden — royalties, carbon pricing, resource levies, SOE dividends, and corporate income tax.

Production Royalties

Ad valorem or per-tonne charges on coal extracted. The most common coal-specific fiscal instrument.

Australia, India, Indonesia, USA, Colombia, South Africa

Carbon Pricing

ETS or carbon tax applied to CO2 from coal combustion. Creates implicit per-tonne cost on coal.

EU ETS, UK Carbon Price Support, South Africa carbon tax

Resource / Cess Taxes

Dedicated levies on coal production or consumption, often earmarked. Separate from royalties.

India GST compensation cess, China resource tax, DMF/NMET

SOE Dividends & Income Tax

Government revenue from owning coal assets (dividends, profit transfers) and corporate income tax on coal companies.

Coal India, China Shenhua/China Energy, Eskom

Methodology & Sources

Revenue Estimation

  • Reported revenue: Where governments publish coal royalty/tax revenue figures (e.g., Australian state treasury data, US ONRR, India ministry reports), these are used directly and tagged REPORTED.
  • Calculated estimates: Where only statutory rates and production data are available, revenue is estimated as: Production (Mt) × Average price ($/t) × Rate (%). These are tagged CALC with the formula shown.
  • Carbon pricing: Coal's share of ETS/carbon tax revenue is estimated from coal's share of covered emissions × total scheme revenue. This is necessarily approximate.
  • All figures converted to USD at 2023 average exchange rates (1 AUD = 0.66 USD; 1 INR = 0.012 USD; 1 EUR = 1.08 USD; 1 ZAR = 0.055 USD).

Scope

  • Included: Production royalties, carbon pricing (ETS/carbon tax), resource/cess taxes, severance taxes, export duties, SOE dividends, and corporate income tax from coal companies.
  • Excluded: General import duties on coal, general VAT/GST (except India's coal-specific GST compensation cess), and indirect economic benefits.
