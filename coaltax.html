Types of Coal Taxation

Countries tax coal through a variety of instruments. These can overlap — a single country may impose royalties, a carbon tax, and export duties simultaneously.

Royalties

Charge on the value or volume of coal extracted. The most common coal-specific fiscal instrument globally.

Australia, Indonesia, USA, Colombia, South Africa

Carbon Pricing

Emissions trading (ETS) or carbon tax applied to CO2 from coal combustion. Implicit tax on coal use.

EU ETS, UK CPS, South Africa carbon tax

Resource / Cess Taxes

Specific levy on coal production or consumption, separate from royalties. Often earmarked for clean energy or development.

India coal cess, China resource tax

Severance / Export Duties

State-level production taxes (severance) or duties on coal exports. Revenue often stays with producing regions.

US state severance, Indonesia export levy

Methodology & Sources

Scope

This page covers taxes, royalties, and levies specifically applied to coal production, consumption, or combustion. It includes:

  • Production royalties: Ad valorem or specific-rate charges on extracted coal (e.g., Australian state royalties, US federal coal royalty, Indonesian royalties).
  • Carbon pricing on coal: Revenue from carbon taxes or emissions trading systems attributable to coal combustion (EU ETS, UK Carbon Price Support, South Africa carbon tax). Attribution is based on coal's share of covered emissions.
  • Resource / cess taxes: Dedicated coal-specific levies such as India's coal cess (now GST compensation cess) and China's resource tax on coal.
  • Severance taxes: US state-level taxes on coal extraction (Wyoming, West Virginia, etc.).
  • Export duties: Levies on coal exports (Indonesia's DMO-linked levies).

Exclusions

  • Corporate income tax paid by coal companies (covered on the Government Revenue page).
  • Value-added tax / GST on coal sales (general consumption taxes, not coal-specific).
  • Import duties on coal.

Revenue Estimation Approach

  • Where official revenue figures are published (Australia, India, USA), these are used directly.
  • Where only rates and production volumes are available, revenue is estimated as: Rate × Production volume × Average price (for ad valorem royalties).
  • Carbon pricing revenue attributed to coal uses: Coal emissions share of total ETS/carbon tax covered emissions × Total scheme revenue.
  • All figures converted to USD at 2023 average exchange rates.

Key Sources

  • World Bank Carbon Pricing Dashboard (carbon tax and ETS data)
  • Queensland Treasury — Royalty revenue data (annual budget papers)
  • NSW Resources Regulator — Mining royalty reports
  • India Ministry of Coal / GST Council — Coal cess collections
  • US ONRR (Office of Natural Resources Revenue) — Federal coal royalties
  • Indonesia ESDM (Ministry of Energy) — Mining royalty data
  • EU ETS data from European Environment Agency / ICAP
  • IEA Coal Information 2024; Energy Institute Statistical Review 2024
  • National tax authority publications for Colombia, South Africa, China, Poland, Germany, Kazakhstan
  • OECD Inventory of Support Measures for Fossil Fuels; IMF carbon pricing data

Caveats

  • Revenue figures are estimates and may differ from official audited accounts.
  • Carbon pricing revenue attribution to coal vs. gas vs. industry is necessarily approximate.
  • Effective tax rates are sensitive to coal prices, exchange rates, and grade mix.
  • Some countries (e.g., China) have province-level variation in resource tax rates; national averages are used.
  • Data years range from 2022 to 2024 depending on source availability.
