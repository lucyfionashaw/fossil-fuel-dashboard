Data gaps: Most countries do not require detailed disclosure of lobbying expenditure. US data (OpenSecrets) is the most complete. Australia requires disclosure of donations above A$16,900 but has high dark money thresholds. The UK Electoral Commission tracks party donations but not lobbying spend directly. EU Transparency Register is voluntary. Estimates for countries like India, China, Russia, and most of the Global South are unavailable.

Methodology & Sources

US Contributions: Data from OpenSecrets (Center for Responsive Politics), which compiles FEC filings. Figures include PAC contributions, individual contributions (employees of fossil fuel companies), and soft money / outside spending where disclosed. "Oil & Gas" includes integrated companies, exploration & production, refining, pipelines, and oilfield services. "Coal Mining" is a separate sector classification.

US Lobbying: Federal lobbying disclosures filed under the Lobbying Disclosure Act (LDA) with the Senate Office of Public Records. Data compiled by OpenSecrets. Figures represent direct lobbying expenditure only and do not include grassroots lobbying, advertising, or public affairs spending.

International Data: Australia: Australian Electoral Commission annual returns. UK: Electoral Commission. Canada: Elections Canada. EU: EU Transparency Register. Global estimates: InfluenceMap "Big Oil's Real Agenda on Climate Change" (2019, updated 2022).

Subsidies Context: IMF "Still Not Getting Energy Prices Right" (2023) for $7T global estimate. US-specific: Oil Change International, Treasury Dept. analysis. Direct subsidies ~$1.3T globally; remainder is unpriced externalities (health, climate, environment).

Limitations: Dark money (501(c)(4) organizations, trade associations) is not fully captured. State-level contributions are excluded from most figures. In-kind contributions and revolving-door benefits are not quantified. International data coverage is highly uneven.
